# Filing with ECC-Net Germany

ECC-Net is the European Consumer Centres Network — a pan-EU system of free cross-border consumer dispute resolution. The German centre (EVZ — Europäisches Verbraucherzentrum Deutschland) handles complaints from German-based consumers against businesses in other EU countries.

---

## When to use ECC-Net Germany

Use ECC-Net Germany when:
- You are based in Germany (or another EU country without a stronger national enforcement body)
- The airline is registered in another EU country (e.g. Spanish, Irish, Dutch)
- The airline has rejected your claim or not responded
- You want a second channel of pressure alongside or instead of the national enforcement body

ECC-Net is particularly useful as a **parallel filing** alongside AESA if you're based in Germany and the airline is Spanish. Two active complaints from two authorities puts more pressure on the airline.

---

## What ECC-Net Germany does

The EVZ acts as an intermediary:
1. They receive your complaint
2. They forward it to the ECC in the airline's home country (e.g. ECC Spain for LEVEL/Vueling)
3. The partner ECC contacts the airline directly
4. The airline typically has 30 days to respond
5. If the airline refuses to engage or rejects the claim, ECC-Net may refer it to a national ADR body (like AESA)

**It is not always binding** — ECC-Net is mediation, not adjudication. But:
- Airlines take it seriously because the ECC can escalate to binding ADR
- It creates a formal record that strengthens your position
- It is entirely free

## How to file

### Online form

Go to: https://eccwebforms.eu/de/germany/complaint

The form is available in German and English. You will need to provide:
- Your personal details (name, address, email)
- The business's details (airline name, country, contact details)
- A description of what happened
- What you claimed and what the airline said
- The amount you are seeking
- Uploaded supporting documents

### Required: Vollmacht (Power of Attorney)

ECC-Net Germany requires you to sign a **Vollmacht** — a document authorizing EVZ to represent you in the dispute. This is a standard form.

Download it from the EVZ website during the complaint process. It must be:
- Filled in with your personal details
- Signed by hand (or with a valid digital signature)
- Uploaded with your complaint

Without the signed Vollmacht, EVZ cannot formally represent you and the complaint may not proceed.

---

## Documents to attach

- Booking confirmation
- The airline's rejection letter (critical — shows EVZ the airline has already refused)
- Receipts for hotel, meals, transport (if claiming Art. 9 care costs)
- Any communications with the airline (emails, chat transcripts)
- Your formal claim letter to the airline (shows you attempted direct resolution first)

The more complete your submission, the less back-and-forth there is with EVZ and the faster they can act.

---

## Timeline expectations

- **Initial acknowledgment**: typically 5–10 business days
- **EVZ contacts ECC in airline's home country**: within 2–3 weeks
- **Airline response window**: ~30 days from ECC contact
- **Resolution**: anywhere from 6 weeks to 6 months depending on complexity and airline cooperation

ECC-Net cases are not as fast as AESA, and the outcome is not always binding. But many airlines settle during this process to avoid escalation to a binding ADR procedure.

---

## Parallel filing: ECC-Net + AESA

If you're based in Germany and the airline is Spanish, you can file with both simultaneously:

1. **File with AESA** (binding ADR for Spanish carriers)
2. **File with ECC-Net Germany** (cross-border mediation)

There is no rule against this. Once you have reference numbers from both:
- Send the airline a short email with both reference numbers
- Airlines dislike having multiple open regulatory files — it creates incentive to settle

---

## After filing

You will receive a case number from EVZ. Keep it and monitor your email for follow-up requests.

If EVZ asks for additional documents, respond promptly — delays on your side extend the timeline.

If the airline engages with EVZ and makes an offer, EVZ will relay it to you. You decide whether to accept. If the offer is lower than your full claim:
- You can counter through EVZ
- Or accept the partial settlement if it covers the most important elements and you want to close the case
- The AESA binding procedure remains open even if ECC-Net mediation fails

## Other EU national enforcement bodies

If you are based in another EU country or the flight departed from a non-German, non-Spanish airport:

| Country | Body | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| UK | CAA (Civil Aviation Authority) | Post-Brexit, enforces UK261/2004. File at caa.co.uk/passengers. |
| France | DGAC | reclamations.dgac@developpement-durable.gouv.fr |
| Germany (for German carriers) | LBA (Luftfahrt-Bundesamt) | Handles Lufthansa, Condor etc. at lba.de |
| Italy | ENAC | enac.gov.it |
| Netherlands | ILT | ilent.nl |
| Ireland | CAR (Commission for Aviation Regulation) | car.ie — Ryanair's home authority |
| Belgium | DGT (Directorate-General for Transport) | dgv.fgov.be |

For Ryanair, filing with CAR in Ireland is often effective because that is where Ryanair is registered, giving CAR direct authority over them.

---

## ECC-Net vs. AESA: which to use first

| Situation | Recommended |
|---|---|
| Spanish carrier (LEVEL, Vueling, Iberia) | AESA first (binding), ECC-Net parallel |
| German carrier (Lufthansa, Condor) from EU airport | LBA |
| Irish carrier (Ryanair) | CAR Ireland, or ECC-Net if based in Germany |
| Non-EU carrier departing EU | ECC-Net of country of departure or claimant |
| Not sure | ECC-Net is always a valid fallback |

# Filing with AESA (Spain)

AESA — Agencia Estatal de Seguridad Aérea — is Spain's civil aviation authority. For claims against Spanish airlines (LEVEL, Vueling, Iberia, Air Europa), it is the single most powerful tool available. Its rulings are legally binding on airlines.

---

## When to use AESA

Use AESA when:
- The airline is Spanish-registered: LEVEL, Vueling, Iberia, Air Europa, Volotea (Spain), Binter Canarias
- The flight departed from a Spanish airport (even if the airline is non-Spanish)
- The airline has rejected your claim with an argument you believe is incorrect
- The airline has not responded within 30 days of your formal claim

You do NOT need to be based in Spain. A claimant in Germany filing against a Spanish carrier can use AESA.

---

## What makes AESA valuable

1. **Binding rulings**: AESA's resolution is legally binding on the airline. If AESA says they owe you €600, they must pay or challenge through administrative courts.
2. **Free**: No cost to file a complaint.
3. **Airline is on the clock**: Once AESA opens a file, the airline has 30 days to respond. Non-response counts against them.
4. **Spanish carriers know it**: LEVEL and Vueling are well aware that AESA rulings cannot be ignored the way individual emails can.

---

## The ADR02 procedure

AESA handles passenger rights complaints under their ADR02 (Alternative Dispute Resolution) procedure. Here's the timeline:

1. **You file the complaint** (via web form or email)
2. **AESA acknowledges receipt** — usually within a few days
3. **AESA may contact you for additional documentation** — you have ~10 days to respond
4. **AESA notifies the airline** — the airline has 30 days to respond
5. **AESA reviews both sides** and issues a resolution
6. **Resolution is binding** — if the airline doesn't comply, AESA can impose fines

Total timeline is typically 2–6 months from filing.

---

## What to prepare

Before filing, gather:
- Your full name and contact address
- Booking reference and flight details (flight numbers, dates, scheduled and actual times)
- Description of what happened, in chronological order
- Copy of the airline's rejection letter (if you have one)
- Receipts for hotel, meals, transport, or other care costs
- Any airline communications (emails, SMS messages about changes)
- Your boarding pass or proof of travel, if available

The more documentation you provide upfront, the faster AESA can resolve the case.

---

## How to file

### Option 1: Online form (recommended)

Go to: https://sede.seguridadaerea.gob.es

1. Navigate to "Reclamaciones de pasajeros" or search for "ADR02"
2. Fill in the online form with your personal details and flight information
3. Describe the disruption and what the airline has done (or not done)
4. Upload supporting documents
5. The form generates a signed PDF for submission

The form is in Spanish. If you don't read Spanish, use Chrome's built-in translation or ask Claude to help you navigate it.

### Option 2: Email

Send a complaint in PDF format to: **sau.aesa@seguridadaerea.es**

The PDF should be:
- Signed (typed name may not be accepted — use a handwritten signature scanned, or a digital signature)
- In Spanish if possible, though AESA does process complaints in English — it just takes longer

Use the template in `templates/aesa-complaint.md` as the basis for your email complaint.

---

## The complaint document: what to include

Your complaint to AESA should cover these sections:

**1. Facts**
- Flight number(s), scheduled dates and times
- What disruption occurred and when you were informed
- What alternative transportation you were offered and when
- What care (hotel, meals) the airline did or did not provide

**2. Claim history**
- When you first contacted the airline
- What the airline responded (include the rejection letter)
- Why the rejection is incorrect (specific articles)

**3. Legal basis**
- Cite Art. 5 (cancellation) and Art. 7 (compensation amounts)
- If care costs are claimed, cite Art. 9
- If the airline is the marketing carrier and blaming the operating carrier, cite Art. 13

**4. Amount claimed**
- Break it down: Art. 7 statutory amount + Art. 9 care costs + any Art. 12 consequential losses
- Include total

**5. What you're asking for**
- State clearly: you are requesting AESA to order the airline to pay [total amount]

## After filing

Once AESA opens the file, you'll receive a case reference number. Keep it.

If AESA asks for additional documents, respond within the deadline they give (usually 10 days). Missing this deadline can result in the case being closed.

**Notify the airline**: Once you have a case reference, send the airline a short email letting them know you've filed with AESA and including the reference number. Many airlines settle before the AESA ruling to avoid the formal process.

---

## If AESA rules in your favor and the airline doesn't pay

AESA's ruling is legally binding. If the airline ignores it:
- AESA can impose administrative fines on the airline
- You can also take the ruling to a civil court as evidence in a small claims action
- In practice, Spanish airlines rarely ignore AESA rulings because the consequences escalate quickly

---

## Common mistakes to avoid

- **Not signing the complaint**: AESA requires a signed document. An unsigned submission may be rejected or delayed.
- **Filing before claiming from the airline**: AESA typically wants to see that you tried to resolve it directly first. Send your claim to the airline, get a response (or wait 30 days), then file with AESA.
- **Not including the rejection letter**: If the airline rejected you and you're disputing the rejection, AESA needs to see their reasoning.
- **Vague description of the disruption**: Be specific. "My flight was disrupted" is not enough. "Flight IB1234 scheduled to depart BER at 06:30 on [DATE] was cancelled; I was placed on VY1234 departing from TXL at 22:00" is what AESA needs.

# Counter-Arguments: How to Defeat Common Airline Rejections

Airlines reject valid claims more often than they should. This guide covers the six most common rejection tactics and the precise legal counter-argument for each one.

Each section includes: what the airline says, why it's wrong (or when it's actually right), the specific article to cite, and suggested language for your response.

---

## 1. "You arrived earlier than scheduled, so no compensation is owed"

**What the airline is doing**: Applying the Art. 6 delay logic to what was actually an Art. 5 cancellation.

**When this argument is actually valid**: Only when the original flight operated and the passenger is making a delay claim under Art. 6. If you arrived at your destination without a delay exceeding the threshold (2h/3h/4h depending on route distance), there is no Art. 6 compensation.

**When this argument is wrong**: Whenever the original scheduled flight did not operate. If your flight was cancelled and you were rebooked on a different flight that arrived earlier — that is still a cancellation under Art. 5. "Earlier arrival" is not a defense to a cancellation claim.

**The legal basis**: Art. 5 sets out the conditions for the cancellation exemption. None of those conditions say "passenger arrived before original scheduled arrival time." The exemption requires the airline to have offered rebooking departing no more than 1 hour before original departure (Art. 5(1)(c)(iii)). Arrival time is not the test.

**Counter-argument language**:
> "I refer to your rejection dated [date]. The rejection states that compensation is not owed because I arrived at my destination earlier than the original scheduled arrival time. This argument applies only to delay claims under Article 6, not to cancellation claims under Article 5. The flight [original flight number] was not operated; accordingly, Article 5 applies. Under Article 5(1)(c)(iii), the compensation exemption requires that the rerouting offered departed no more than one hour before the original scheduled departure. The alternative routing I was placed on departed [X hours] before the original scheduled departure, which does not satisfy the Article 5(1)(c)(iii) exemption. I therefore maintain my claim for €[amount] under Article 7(1)(c)."

## 2. "The disruption was an extraordinary circumstance (weather, ATC, etc.)"

**What the airline is doing**: Invoking the Art. 5(3) defense to avoid the statutory compensation.

**When this argument is actually valid**: When the disruption was genuinely caused by an event outside the airline's control that could not have been avoided even with all reasonable measures taken. Examples: volcanic ash cloud closing airspace, severe weather making flight genuinely unsafe, unforeseeable ATC strikes, political unrest at the destination.

**When this argument is wrong**:
- Crew rostering problems or strikes by the airline's own staff
- Technical problems with the aircraft, unless caused by a hidden manufacturing defect that could not have been detected (narrow exception — most technical issues do not qualify)
- Aircraft substitution due to the airline's commercial scheduling decisions
- The airline claiming weather was the cause when weather only affected a different route

**Critical point**: Even if the extraordinary circumstances argument is valid for the Art. 7 compensation, it does NOT defeat the Art. 9 care claim (meals, hotel, transport). Those are owed regardless.

**Counter-argument language**:
> "You have cited extraordinary circumstances as grounds to refuse compensation under Article 7. I note that [specific reason — e.g. 'the disruption was caused by a crew rostering issue, which constitutes an operational matter within the airline's control and does not qualify as an extraordinary circumstance under Art. 5(3)']. Furthermore, regardless of whether extraordinary circumstances apply to the Art. 7 statutory compensation, the right to care under Article 9 is independent and applies in all cases of cancellation requiring hotel accommodation. I am therefore maintaining my claim for care costs of €[amount] under Article 9 even if you dispute the Art. 7 amount."

## 3. "The disruption was caused by the operating carrier — please contact them directly"

**What the airline is doing**: Attempting to redirect the passenger to the airline that physically flew the plane, rather than the airline that sold the ticket.

**Why this is always wrong**: Article 13 of the regulation explicitly states that the marketing carrier (the carrier that sold the ticket) bears full liability to the passenger. The marketing carrier's right to seek redress from the operating carrier is between them — it is not the passenger's concern and does not affect where the passenger must direct their claim.

**Counter-argument language**:
> "Under Article 13 of Regulation (EC) 261/2004, liability to the passenger rests with the marketing carrier — in this case, [airline name] — regardless of which operating carrier was responsible for the disruption. The regulation explicitly provides that the marketing carrier may seek redress from the operating carrier, but this arrangement between carriers does not affect my right to claim against [airline name] as the carrier with whom I have a contract and who bears full regulatory liability. I am therefore maintaining my claim against [airline name] and request a substantive response to the compensation calculation in my original claim."

## 4. "We rerouted you to an alternative flight — this constitutes assistance under the regulation"

**What the airline is doing**: Claiming that rerouting alone satisfies their obligations.

**Why this is often wrong**: Rerouting satisfies the Art. 8 obligation (choice between refund or rerouting). It does NOT satisfy Art. 7 (compensation), which is a separate entitlement triggered by the cancellation. The Art. 5(1)(c)(iii) exemption from compensation requires specific timing conditions on the rerouting.

**What to check**: Did the rerouting depart no more than 1 hour before the original AND arrive no more than 2 hours after the original? If not, the compensation exemption does not apply, even though the rerouting itself was provided.

**Counter-argument language**:
> "I acknowledge that [airline name] provided rerouting under Article 8. However, the provision of rerouting does not extinguish the right to compensation under Article 7. Compensation is separate from rerouting. The exemption from compensation under Article 5(1)(c)(iii) requires that the alternative routing departs no more than one hour before the original scheduled departure AND arrives no more than two hours after the original scheduled arrival. The routing I was placed on departed at [time] — [X hours] before the original scheduled departure of [time]. This does not satisfy Article 5(1)(c)(iii), and Article 7 compensation therefore remains due."

## 5. "You accepted the new flight, which means you accepted the arrangement"

**What the airline is doing**: Arguing that accepting a rebooked flight constitutes a waiver of compensation rights.

**Why this is wrong**: Passengers in a disruption have no practical choice but to accept the available transport to their destination. Accepting the only way to get home is not a waiver of legal rights. The regulation was specifically designed to protect passengers in asymmetric situations — airlines cannot use the fact of rebooking acceptance as a waiver.

There is no provision in EC 261/2004 that says accepting rerouting waives compensation. Courts across the EU have consistently held this.

**Counter-argument language**:
> "Accepting the alternative transportation you provided was not a waiver of my rights under Regulation (EC) 261/2004. The regulation grants passengers the right to both rerouting (Art. 8) and compensation (Art. 7) — these are separate entitlements, and accepting one does not forfeit the other. No provision of the regulation provides that accepting a rerouting extinguishes the right to compensation. I therefore maintain my claim in full."

---

## 6. "We notified you of the change in advance, so the regulation does not apply"

**What the airline is doing**: Claiming they gave sufficient notice to trigger the Art. 5(1)(c) exemption.

**What to check**: The exemption has specific thresholds:
- Notified ≥14 days before departure: full exemption
- Notified 7–13 days before departure AND rerouting offers specific time windows: partial exemption
- Notified <7 days before departure AND rerouting departs ≤1h before original AND arrives ≤2h after original: exemption applies

If notification was less than 2 weeks before departure, the exemption requires very specific rerouting conditions that must be verified.

**Counter-argument language** (for notification under 2 weeks):
> "Your claim that you provided advance notification does not satisfy the Article 5(1)(c) exemption in this case. The notification was provided [X days] before departure, which falls within the period governed by Art. 5(1)(c)(iii) — requiring that the rerouting offered departed no more than one hour before the original and arrived no more than two hours after the original. The rerouting I received departed [X hours] before the original scheduled departure, which does not meet this condition. The exemption therefore does not apply and compensation remains due."

## How to use these arguments

1. Identify which argument the airline is making (often there are multiple)
2. Look up the corresponding section above
3. Copy the counter-argument language into your response letter, substituting the details in brackets
4. Always cite the specific article numbers — it signals that you know the regulation
5. Set a 14-day response deadline and state that you will escalate to AESA/relevant authority if not resolved

Airlines bank on most passengers accepting their rejections. A response that cites specific article numbers and demands specific answers forces a substantive legal engagement — and many airlines will settle rather than sustain a regulatory complaint.

# Your Rights Under EC 261/2004

EU Regulation 261/2004 gives you specific, enforceable rights when your flight is cancelled, significantly delayed, or you are denied boarding. This guide explains what those rights are, when they apply, and what airlines are legally obligated to do — versus what they often claim.

---

## When the regulation applies

The regulation applies to you if:
- Your flight **departs from an EU airport** (regardless of which airline)
- Your flight **arrives at an EU airport on an EU airline** (if departing from outside the EU)

It also covers the **UK** under retained EU law (UK261/2004), **Norway**, **Iceland**, and **Switzerland** by treaty.

If you're flying Berlin → Barcelona → San Francisco on a single ticket, the whole journey is covered because the first leg departs from an EU airport.

---

## Three situations that trigger compensation

### 1. Cancellation

Your flight was cancelled and you were either given a refund or put on a different flight.

**What you're owed:**
- The choice between a refund (full ticket price) or rerouting to your destination
- If rerouting: meals and refreshments while waiting, hotel if overnight stay required
- **€250/€400/€600 in statutory compensation** (see amounts below)

**When the €250/€400/€600 is NOT owed:**
The airline only escapes compensation if ALL of the following are true:
- They informed you of the cancellation at least 2 weeks before departure, OR
- They offered you a rerouting departing no more than 2 hours before the original and arriving no more than 4 hours after original, OR
- They offered you a rerouting departing no more than **1 hour before** the original and arriving no more than **2 hours after** original

Note: all three of these are alternatives (OR conditions). The most common one airlines misapply is the third bullet — if they rebooked you on a flight that left 2+ hours before your original departure, the exemption does NOT apply.

### 2. Significant delay

Your original flight operated but departed or arrived late.

**Compensation thresholds:**
- Flights ≤1500km: delay of **2+ hours** at destination
- Flights >1500km intra-EU, or 1500–3500km other: delay of **3+ hours** at destination
- Flights >3500km: delay of **4+ hours** at destination

For delays, the "earlier arrival" defense does apply: if you arrived at your destination within the threshold, no compensation is due under Art. 6. But if the flight was cancelled (not just delayed), Art. 6 does not apply — see "Cancellation" above.

### 3. Denied boarding

You had a confirmed reservation and the airline refused to let you on the plane (usually due to overbooking).

**What you're owed:**
- Same compensation as cancellation (€250/€400/€600)
- The choice between refund or rerouting
- Meals, hotel, transport (same as cancellation)

The airline must first ask for volunteers. If no one volunteers and you are involuntarily bumped, full compensation applies.

---

## Compensation amounts

These are fixed statutory amounts per passenger:

| Route | Amount |
|---|---|
| Flights ≤1,500 km | €250 |
| Intra-EU flights >1,500 km | €400 |
| Non-EU flights 1,500–3,500 km | €400 |
| All other flights (intercontinental) | **€600** |

These amounts apply per passenger. If you were traveling with 3 people on the same booking, each person is owed separately — total €1,800 for a transatlantic cancellation.

The airline can reduce the Art. 7 amount by 50% if they rebooked you and you arrived within certain time windows. But only in very specific circumstances — and those circumstances require the new departure to be close to the original.

## Care costs: the right airlines most often forget to mention

Under Article 9, when there is a cancellation or long delay, the airline must provide — **regardless of the cause of the disruption:**

- **Meals and refreshments** in reasonable relation to the waiting time
- **Hotel accommodation** if an overnight stay is necessary
- **Transport** between the airport and hotel
- **Two phone calls, emails, or faxes** to inform people of your situation

**These are owed even if the disruption was caused by extraordinary circumstances** (bad weather, air traffic control strike, etc.). The "extraordinary circumstances" defense only affects whether you're owed the statutory €250/€400/€600 — it does not affect Art. 9 care costs.

If the airline didn't provide these at the airport, you can claim your actual out-of-pocket expenses retrospectively. Keep receipts.

---

## The "extraordinary circumstances" defense

Airlines invoke this to avoid paying the statutory compensation (Art. 7). It applies to:
- Extreme weather affecting safety
- Air traffic control strikes
- Security incidents
- Medical emergencies involving passengers
- Political instability at the destination

It does NOT apply to:
- Technical problems (unless caused by hidden manufacturing defects that couldn't be anticipated — a very narrow exception)
- Crew shortages or rostering problems
- Aircraft swaps due to operational reasons
- Problems caused by the airline's own commercial decisions

Even when the defense applies, you are still owed care costs (meals, hotel) and the right to rerouting or refund.

---

## The marketing vs. operating carrier issue

When two different airlines are involved in your journey — one that sold the ticket and one that flew the plane — the airline that sold you the ticket (the "marketing carrier") is fully liable to you under the regulation.

This matters when:
- You booked through LEVEL, but Vueling operated your first leg
- You booked with British Airways, but Iberia flew the route as a codeshare

The marketing carrier cannot tell you to go claim from the operating carrier. The regulation puts liability on whoever sold you the ticket. What happens between the marketing carrier and operating carrier is their internal problem.

## Time limits for claims

Claim time limits vary by country (they are governed by national law, not by EC 261/2004 itself):

| Country | Time limit |
|---|---|
| Germany | 3 years |
| UK | 6 years |
| France | 5 years |
| Spain | 1 year (general) |
| Netherlands | 2 years |

When in doubt, file as soon as possible. The longer you wait, the harder it becomes to produce documentation.

---

## How to actually get paid

Most airlines have an online claims form. Use it as the initial step. But:

- Keep a record of everything you submit
- Note the claim reference number
- If you get a rejection, read it carefully — many are based on incorrect legal arguments (see `guides/counter-arguments.md`)
- If the airline ignores you or rejects incorrectly, escalate to the national enforcement body (see `guides/aesa.md` and `guides/eccnet.md`)

Enforcement authority complaints are free. Airlines take them seriously because adverse rulings are binding and create public records. Many claims that go ignored for months settle quickly once AESA or another authority gets involved.
